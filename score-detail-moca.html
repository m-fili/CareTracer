What this score does not capture

Daily living function Whether you can manage cooking, shopping, finances, and other activities at home
Mood and depression Symptoms of depression can affect cognitive testing but are evaluated separately
Sleep patterns Poor sleep can lower scores temporarily; sleep is not measured here
Behavioral changes Personality or behavioral shifts often noticed by family members are not in the score
Performance during typical activities Office testing is a snapshot; how you function day-to-day is captured differently
Subtle changes in highly-educated individuals MoCA has ceiling effects; small declines from a previously high baseline may not show up

MoCA is a screening tool, not a diagnostic test on its own. Your neurology team uses it together with other assessments and your history.

Your score has moved from 26 to 24 to 22 across three measurements over 19 months. The trend is consistent with the slow, gradual change your neurology team has documented since your initial MCI assessment in August 2024. A single MoCA score is a snapshot, and changes of one to two points between visits can also reflect testing variability, attention, sleep, or how a particular day was going.

Why this matters for your care

Your MoCA trajectory is one of several inputs your neurology team uses to track your cognitive health and to decide whether and when to adjust your care. Other inputs include the full neuropsychological battery from February 2025, your brain MRI, your daily-function reports, and conversations with you and your family. The next neurology visit is a good moment to review the trajectory together, discuss how things are going at home, and revisit the advance-care-planning conversation noted in your last neurology summary.
